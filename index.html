About Kal-Polymers

Kal-Polymers is a prominent recycler and compounder of post-industrial plastics, headquartered in Mississauga, Ontario. With a commitment to sustainability, the company specializes in converting industrial plastic waste into high-quality recycled polymers used across various industries, including automotive, packaging, and consumer goods.

  • Promoting Environmental Sustainability

    By recycling and repurposing industrial plastic waste, Kal-Polymers prevents millions of kilograms of plastics from ending up in landfills, reducing environmental pollution and conserving natural resources.

  • Driving Economic Growth

    The company plays a vital role in the circular economy by creating valuable materials from waste, supporting manufacturers with cost-effective, sustainable raw materials, and generating employment in the recycling sector.

  • Supporting Global Industries

    Kal-Polymers provides innovative solutions to industries such as automotive and packaging, helping them meet their sustainability goals while maintaining product quality and performance.

Junior Technical Analyst — roles and responsibilities

I sat on the SharePoint team supporting MTO and MLITSD for SharePoint Online, Power Automate, and Power Apps (Canvas Apps). MLITSD was my primary assignment, while I still picked up meaningful exposure to MTO work to understand how both ministries operated.

  • SharePoint delivery
  • Power Automate & Power Apps
  • Meetings & requirements
  • Team rhythm & tooling
  • Volume & prioritization

SharePoint & information architecture

Standing up workable spaces so clients can collaborate with confidence.

Our team assembled the foundational SharePoint sites and file structures ministries requested—from navigation and libraries to patterns that scaled as content grew. A critical part of the role was translating “what they need” into sustainable structures teams could adopt without rework.

Permissions and controlled access were centrally important: delegated administration meant our group was relied on to implement and tune access controls so sensitive material stayed with the right audiences across programs and portfolios.

Automation & canvas solutions

Supporting MLITSD with Power Platform where SharePoint alone is not enough.

I contributed to Power Automate flows and Canvas apps requested by MLITSD stakeholders. Detail about specific applications stays high-level given confidentiality, but day to day work involved wiring together approvals, notifications, lists, documents, and user experiences tailored to ministry staff—not generic templates dropped in place.

Alongside delivery, our team sustained the existing portfolio of sites, flows, and apps—patching logic, extending features, closing gaps surfaced by operational change, and helping clients understand what was possible within policy and governance guardrails.

Client meetings & requirements

Where delivery quality is decided before a single line of configuration ships.

Beyond internal agile ceremonies, I joined client-facing sessions—often several in a day—to report progress, demo incremental value, and capture requirements as problems evolved. Those conversations demanded clarity: translating informal asks into deliverable scope, confirming assumptions, and signaling trade-offs early so expectations stayed aligned.

That cadence shaped how I prepared for meetings, how I followed up in writing, and how I learned to ask the “obvious” clarifying questions that prevent expensive rework later.

Team cadence & Microsoft ecosystem

Structure that keeps dozens of parallel threads from turning into noise.

We held three stand-ups per week and a biweekly sprint-style meeting to surface risks, align on priorities, and sequence work across the team. Those touchpoints mattered because the SharePoint team supported a large, varied backlog spanning both ministries.

Day-to-day coordination lived in the Microsoft stack: OneNote for shared documentation, Azure DevOps for tickets and traceability, and the familiar M365 collaboration surface that clients already knew. Staying inside that ecosystem reduced friction for handoffs and reviews.

Many projects, one calendar

Time management as a core job skill, not a side note.

The team juggled dozens of concurrent efforts, each with its own stakeholders, urgency, and technical dependencies. Learning to triage—what blocks others, what can batch, what needs a quick client ping—became as important as tool proficiency.

That environment rewarded disciplined planning: protecting focus time for build work, keeping tickets current, and communicating early when dates slipped so leads could reprioritize without surprises.

Goals and Reflections

University learning objectives mapped to GovTech delivery—oral communication, technical learning, and time management.

Learning goal 1

Communicating — oral communication

Improve clarity when explaining technical ideas to clients and teammates during requirements discussions and demos of SharePoint and Power Platform solutions.

Action plan
  • Participate actively in client meetings and ask clarifying questions when requirements are fuzzy.
  • Practice translating Power Apps and Automate concepts into plain language.
  • Request feedback from my supervisor and peers on explanations and demos.
  • Take opportunities to walk through workflows or features I contributed to.
Measures of success
  • Add value in client discussions with relevant requirement questions.
  • Deliver at least one walkthrough of a workflow or solution to a client or teammate.
  • Hear constructive, positive signals on clarity from my supervisor or team.

Reflection

Client meetings rewarded preparation and repetition. Early on I over-explained internals; feedback and practice helped me lead with outcomes (“what changes for your team”) before mechanics. Asking clarification questions at the end of agendas—not only at kickoff—reduced ambiguity and made demos feel collaborative rather than performative. By term’s end I was comfortable owning short walkthroughs and following up with written summaries that matched what we had verbally agreed—an oral communication habit I will keep in any technical role.

Learning goal 2

Critical & creative thinking — inquiry & analysis

Build the ability to pick up unfamiliar Power Platform features and patterns quickly so I can deliver sound solutions under real ministry constraints.

Action plan
  • Use Microsoft learn content, documentation, and examples when requirements outpace prior experience.
  • Experiment in non-production paths to test connectors, expressions, and UI patterns safely.
  • Ask targeted questions of senior teammates when documentation alone is not enough.
  • Capture reusable notes on what worked to speed up the next similar request.
Measures of success
  • Apply at least two new techniques or features successfully during the term.
  • Show I can research and implement unfamiliar tasks with limited hand-holding.
  • Maintain a personal log of lessons learned and patterns worth reusing.

Reflection

Power Platform rewards structured experimentation: read the doc, build a tiny proof, then scale. I learned to time-box spikes so client deadlines did not suffer while I was still learning. Pairing quick research with screenshots or short recorded walkthroughs for my team turned private experiments into shared knowledge. Looking back, the biggest shift was confidence—I stopped treating “I have not done this exact thing” as a blocker and started treating it as a bounded learning task with a checklist.

Learning goal 3

Professional & ethical behaviour — organization & time management

Manage a mix of build work, break-fix requests, and recurring client touchpoints without letting priorities slip.

Action plan
  • Track tasks, due dates, and waiting-on-others states in notes and DevOps work items.
  • Split large deliverables into steps I can finish and demo incrementally.
  • Surface blockers to my supervisor early with enough context to help.
  • Order work by client impact, risk, and dependencies—not only by who asked last.
Measures of success
  • Routinely finish assigned work inside the expected window—or reset dates proactively.
  • Stay organized when multiple clients pull in parallel.
  • Receive feedback that my workload management supports the wider team.

Reflection

The hardest part was context switching: inbox items, sprint commitments, and same-day meetings all competed for attention. A simple habit—ending each stand-up block with five minutes to reprioritize the rest of my calendar—prevented reactive firefighting. Breaking stories into tangible increments also made estimation honest; supervisors could see momentum even when surprises appeared. Honest escalation turned out to be part of good time management: admitting unknowns earlier cost less than delivering late explanations.
